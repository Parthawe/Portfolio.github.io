Jugalbandi

Overview

A duet in Hindi

Jugalbandi (meaning “a duet” in Hindi) showcases a unique blend of traditional analog mechanical instruments and modern digital MIDI instruments. The project is a physical computing art installation that materialises the inner workings of neural networks into tangible, playable musical instruments.

01 — Background

The Concept

The genesis of this project drew inspiration from the ‘Abacus’ project undertaken at panGenerator Studio. While the Abacus focused on recognizing digits, Jugalbandi operates on a similar concept but transforms neural network processes into acoustic and electronic sound.

The installation is a 1:1 interactive physical representation of a real, functioning deep learning network, manifested through musical instruments rather than visual displays.

Inspiration panGenerator Studio — “The Abacus”
Approach Translate neural network layers into tangible, playable acoustic and electronic mechanisms

02 — Research

Materialise Neural Networks

Deep learning networks are a type of artificial neural network (ANN) with multiple layers of processing units. Each layer learns to perform a specific task, and the layers work together to learn complex patterns in data. Jugalbandi maps these layers to physical sound-making mechanisms — each layer of the network controls a different instrument or actuator.

Original artform inspired by panGenerator Studio — called “The Abacus,” which uses digits as the final output. Jugalbandi replaces digits with musical notes.

03 — The Instrument

Hexa-18

Hexa-18 is a custom-built hexagonal instrument that combines three distinct sound-making mechanisms into a single sculptural form — wind, string, and percussion. Each face of the polyhedron serves a different acoustic or electronic function.

Wind
Air-driven pipes of varying heights produce different tonal frequencies, controlled by solenoid valves.
String
Servo-actuated plucking mechanisms produce melodic sequences through algorithmically composed patterns.
Percussion
Speaker cones and vibration motors embedded in hexagonal faces create rhythmic, percussive textures.
Sculpture
The hexagonal form is both a visual art piece and a functional instrument — each face serves a purpose.

04 — Components

Mechanized Harp

The mechanized harp is built inside a wooden frame and uses servo motors to pluck individual strings automatically. An Arduino Mega controls the timing and sequencing of each pluck, allowing for algorithmically composed melodies.

Controller Mega Arduino
Actuators Servo Motors
Materials Wire Mesh, Harp, Wood Frame

05 — Component

Automated Flute

The automated flute uses an air pump to push air through the blowing hole, while servo motors actuated by Lego mechanisms open and close the finger holes to produce different notes. The entire assembly is controlled by a Mega Arduino.

Air SourceAir Pump
ActuatorsServo Motors
ControllerMega Arduino
StructureLegos, Clamp, Blowing Hole

06 — Component

Rainsticks

The rainstick module is mounted on a wooden structure and uses servo motors to tilt and rotate cardboard rainsticks, producing cascading percussive sounds. A dedicated circuit board coordinates the timing of each tilt to layer rhythmic patterns.

Actuators Servo Motors
Structure Wooden Frame
Sound Source Rainsticks
Electronics Circuit Board

08 — Reflection

My Role

In Jugalbandi, my role was pivotal in translating the complex workings of artificial neural networks into an interactive physical installation. I navigated through challenges while utilizing tools like TensorFlow, ML5.JS, and Teachable Machines. Collaborating with mentors, I contributed to creating a dataset tailored specifically for the project, overcoming hurdles in model accuracy, and adapting methodologies to ensure the successful convergence of art and science in the final performance.
