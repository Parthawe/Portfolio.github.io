The Vision

Smart glasses have been a graveyard of good intentions. Google Glass, Snap Spectacles, North Focals — all shipped impressive hardware and then asked users to figure out why they should wear it. Mentra Glass answers that question with an open-source OS and the first miniapp store for smart glasses — turning a piece of hardware into a platform people put on every morning and never take off.

My Role

As Head of UI/UX, I own every design surface: companion app, MentraOS on-glasses interface, MiniApp Store, developer experience, and the design system binding it all. I work across hardware constraints, AI interaction patterns, and developer tooling — translating an ambitious product vision into interfaces that feel obvious the first time you use them.

Context

A Decade of Expensive Failures

Before designing forward, I studied backward. Smart glasses have a decade-long trail of ambitious launches and quiet discontinuations. The pattern never varies: a hardware company ships something technically impressive, bundles a companion app with three features, and waits for the “ecosystem” to magically appear. It never does.

Google Glass had the sensors but no software story. Snap Spectacles had the brand but no utility past fifteen-second clips. North Focals had the elegance but no reason to exist past the first week. Every one treated software as an afterthought and developers as an audience that would show up uninvited.

The lesson: smart glasses without an ecosystem are an expensive accessory with a charging cable.

“The smartphone won because of the App Store. Smart glasses will too.”

The Bet

What Android Did for Phones, MentraOS Does for Glasses

Mentra’s thesis is simple and radical: smart glasses need an open-source OS and a developer marketplace to become a daily-wear platform. Not a better camera. Not a lighter frame. An ecosystem.

MentraOS is that ecosystem — open-source, community-driven, designed from the ground up for face-worn computing. The MiniApp Store gives developers a place to publish, users a place to discover, and the platform a reason to grow beyond what any single company could build alone.

The bet: the smart glasses that win will not have the best specs on paper. They will have the best app on your face — built by someone you have never met, for a problem only you have, found in a store that runs on your glasses.

Design Challenges

Four Problems Nobody Had Cracked

Smart glasses are not “mobile design but smaller.” The screen is in your peripheral vision, the input is voice and gesture, and the context is the real world. Every assumption from a decade of screen design had to be thrown out.

The Screen You Never Look At
The display lives in peripheral vision, not dead center. UI elements must be glanceable, not readable. Hierarchy is about where in the visual field something appears, not font size. I had to unlearn centering content and design for the edges of attention.
Sixty Seconds or You Lose Them
New form factor, zero patience. I designed a four-step onboarding — slide on, download app, pair, say “Hey Mentra” — that gets users from unboxing to first interaction in under a minute. No tutorials, no walkthrough. Wear them and talk.
If They Won’t Build, Nothing Else Matters
An app store is only as good as its apps. I designed the SDK docs, developer portal, and submission flow to make building for glasses feel as natural as building for phones — so developers choose this platform first, not as an afterthought.
A Camera on Your Face Is a Social Contract
Post-Google-Glass, face-worn cameras carry social baggage. The challenge was not hardware weight — it was emotional weight. I designed visual indicators, privacy modes, and interaction patterns that build trust with both the wearer and everyone in the room.
Companion App

The Control Center in Your Pocket

The companion app is not a remote control — it is the bridge between your phone and your face. It handles everything the glasses should not: deep configuration, miniapp management, firmware updates, AI settings. The design rule: anything requiring more than three words of reading happens on the phone, not the glasses.

Pairing was the first interaction I obsessed over. Bluetooth pairing is traditionally a five-minute frustration. I designed a one-tap flow — scan the QR code on the glasses case, connection established, personalized home screen — all in under thirty seconds. The glasses confirm with a haptic pulse and a voice prompt: “Connected. Say Hey Mentra to get started.”

1
Slide On
Unfold the temples and the glasses power on. No buttons, no long-press. The accelerometer detects the motion and boots MentraOS in under two seconds.
2
Download App
A card in the display directs you to download the companion app. NFC in the case triggers the download on Android — one less step between unboxing and using.
3
Pair
Scan the QR code. One tap to confirm. The phone and glasses handshake over Bluetooth LE and the connection is persistent — no re-pairing, no “searching for device.”
4
“Hey Mentra”
The wake word activates the AI. Directions, translation, song ID, calendar — the first interaction sets the tone: this is not a screen on your face. It is an intelligence you wear.

Beyond onboarding, the app is the management layer for the entire ecosystem: browse miniapps, configure per-app notifications, adjust display settings, manage privacy. I built it on a single-tab architecture — no hamburger menus, no buried settings. Everything is two taps away, because this should feel like a thirty-second tool, not a thirty-minute app.

MentraOS

An OS That Disappears Until You Need It

MentraOS is open-source — a product decision that became the defining design constraint. An open OS means developers will build things I cannot predict, on a display I cannot control, for users I will never meet. The design system had to be opinionated enough to feel cohesive and flexible enough for miniapps that do not exist yet.

The HUD operates on a principle I call “glance, don’t gaze.” Every piece of information must be understood in under two seconds of peripheral attention. Notifications arrive as translucent cards at the edge of vision, auto-dismissing after three seconds unless engaged. No notification center — if you missed it, the companion app has the history.

Voice-First, Screen-Second

Voice is the primary input — not because it is always best, but because your hands are busy living your life. The AI handles natural language queries, contextual responses from the camera feed, and proactive suggestions from time and location. I designed it to follow conversation, not command-line syntax: you say “Hey Mentra, remind me to check the oven in five,” not “Mentra, set timer five minutes.”

Visual feedback is minimal by design: a thin amber waveform that pulses while listening and settles when processing. No full-screen takeover, no animated avatar, no spinner. The glasses should feel barely there — until you need them.

Notification Architecture

On a phone, a badge sits quietly until you look. On glasses, every notification competes with reality. I designed three tiers: ambient (subtle color shift at the frame edge), informational (translucent one-line card), and urgent (persistent card with haptic pulse requiring voice dismissal). Users assign tiers per app, and MentraOS learns from behavior — consistently ignore an app within one second and it auto-downgrades to ambient.

MiniApp Store

The App Store That Lives on Your Face

This is what separates Mentra from everything else. Meta Ray-Ban Gen 2 ships at the same $299 price point but is a closed system — no app store, no third-party development, no ecosystem growth beyond what Meta decides to build. Mentra is the opposite: an open marketplace where any developer can ship.

Designing a store for a HUD meant rethinking every convention. No icon grid. No screenshot carousel. The on-glasses store is voice-navigated and context-curated — ask for a translation app while looking at a foreign menu, and it surfaces relevant miniapps before you finish the sentence.

Discoverability Without Browsing

On a phone, discovery means browsing. On glasses, discovery means context. The store surfaces miniapps from three signals: what you are doing (accelerometer), where you are (GPS + scene recognition), and what you are asking for (natural language intent). The result feels less like a catalog and more like a knowledgeable friend who always knows the right tool.

Deeper browsing happens in the companion app — categories, reviews, developer profiles, curated collections. I added trust signals specific to wearables: privacy badges showing exactly what data each miniapp accesses, installation size relative to local storage, and battery impact ratings from real-world testing.

Developer Experience

An app store lives or dies by its developers. I designed the portal and SDK docs with the same care as the consumer product. Submission is three steps: upload, metadata, review. The docs follow a “first miniapp in 15 minutes” philosophy — zero to working hello-world in a single sitting. Pre-built UI components auto-conform to the MentraOS design system, so developers ship faster without worrying about HUD rendering.

Reflections

What Building for the Face Taught Me

  • World-first design is a different discipline. On a phone, the screen is the world. On glasses, the world is the screen. Every decision starts with one question: does this help the user engage with reality, or pull them away? If it pulls, the design is wrong — no matter how good it looks in Figma.
  • Constraint is the best creative force. A peripheral display, 43 grams, voice-first input — no hover states, no scroll, no tap targets. What remains is the essence of the information, and that essence is usually more powerful than the decorated version I would have built on a phone.
  • Open ecosystems demand opinionated design systems. The more open the platform, the more disciplined the design language must be. I built tokens and components to be strict where consistency matters (typography, spacing, notifications) and flexible where creativity matters (color, layout, animation).
  • Trust is a design material. Privacy indicators, recording lights, clear permissions — these are not features. They are the foundation that makes every other feature possible. Without trust, the best AI in the world is just a creepy camera.
